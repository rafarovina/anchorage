# Manifesto

> Why Anchorage exists, why now, and why this shape rather than another.

## The problem

Cooperative knowledge work is in a strange spot.

On one side, there has never been more material: more papers, more datasets, more preprints, more model-generated summaries of all of the above. On the other side, the structures we use to make sense of that material are failing in opposite directions. Chat-based research evaporates the moment the session closes — what an LLM helped you understand on Tuesday is gone by Friday, with no shared artifact and no way for someone else to build on it. Wikipedia, the closest thing to a working public-knowledge commons, runs governance at a tempo set by humans deliberating in talk pages, which is the right tempo for a 2003 web but the wrong tempo for a world where contributions can be machine-generated by the thousand. Knowledge graphs in academia and industry mostly fail because they're either too coarse (one node per paper) or too unconstrained (one node per opinion, with no curation), and either way they don't accumulate trust.

Meanwhile, the actual work researchers do — assembling claims from sources, checking lineage, identifying gaps, synthesizing across a literature, arguing about what the evidence supports — has gotten dramatically cheaper at the level of an individual making one move. The thing that hasn't gotten cheaper is **trustworthy aggregation of those moves into a shared, durable artifact**.

That gap is the project.

## Why now

Two things changed at once, and they're the same thing.

**Honest individual contribution got cheap.** A motivated person with an LLM can ground a claim, fetch a citation, propose a synthesis, review a peer's reasoning, or notice that two clusters of evidence haven't been bridged — in minutes, at the cost of a few API calls or a local model running on a laptop. The bottleneck for cooperative research stopped being individual capability and became coordination, trust, and curation.

**Adversarial contribution got cheap too.** The same tools that empower honest contributors let bad-faith actors generate plausible-looking citations, fabricate synthesis nodes that subtly bias a contested topic, and run patient long-game campaigns to build reputation before drifting an outcome. Pharma actors, ideological actors, hostile state actors — anyone with a stake in what a public knowledge graph says about their topic — now has tools that let one operator run a small army of contributors at vanishingly low cost.

The cooperative-research projects that would have worked in 2018 don't work in 2026, because their threat models assumed humans on both sides. The systems that would have worked even in 2024 are stretched to their breaking point now. We need to design for this asymmetry from the start, not patch around it later.

The bet underlying Anchorage is that the design that's robust to weak honest contributors is most of the way to being robust against strategic adversaries. Both push toward the same defenses: verifiable anchors enforced at the write path, redundant peer review, calibration batches, staked reputation, and — crucially — exhaustive simulation against an adversarial population before any of it touches a real user.

## The core bet

Almost every cooperative-knowledge project that has tried to be ambitious has failed on one of two opposite problems: **the unit of contribution was wrong**, or **the unit of belonging was wrong**.

Wikipedia's unit of contribution is the article edit, which is too big to verify mechanically and too small to capture an argument. Forum posts are too unstructured to compose. Knowledge-graph triples are too small to carry meaning. Pull requests work for code because code has tests; they don't work for claims because claims don't. So projects that nailed the unit of belonging — *I'm helping write the encyclopedia* — got buried under the impossibility of mechanically curating their unit of contribution at LLM scale.

In the other direction, projects that nailed the unit of contribution — narrow scope envelopes, atomic claims, verifiable lineage — failed at recruitment because the unit of belonging was unrelatable. *I'm helping synthesize ctDNA-MRD evidence for resected stage II CRC* doesn't fit on a poster. The audience is too small, the gravity too weak, the work feels like a niche grant proposal instead of a cause.

Anchorage bets that **the unit of contribution and the unit of belonging are different sizes, and the project's job is to hold them together**.

The unit of contribution is **an atomic claim plus the explicit set of parents from which it derives**. Given a parent set and a proposed child, "is this inferential step valid?" is small, local, and answerable. Two reviewers can disagree, but they disagree *about something specific*, and the disagreement decomposes — into a hidden assumption that should become an explicit parent, a genuine open question that becomes a node, or two parallel synthesis nodes the system carries side by side until evidence resolves them.

The unit of belonging is **a cause** — an umbrella topic large enough that thousands of people across many specialties recognize themselves as caring about it. Colon cancer. Antibiotic resistance. Long-horizon climate response. The cause is what someone joins.

Between the cause and the claim sits a third structure: **sub-topics**, which are scope envelopes inside a cause. *Ctdna-MRD in stage II CRC*, *Lynch surveillance intervals*, *ctDNA-guided de-escalation in stage III* — each tractable, each producible-as-manuscript, each one cluster of work inside the colon-cancer cause. Sub-topics are first-class objects. They are *discovered through the system*, not chosen up front: contributors propose them, the graph state tells the system which proposals are tractable (corpus density, anchor coverage, projected closure distance), good ones attract effort and ship syntheses, bad ones quietly fade. Scope-finding becomes a product feature, not a v0 design decision.

This three-layer structure — cause → sub-topic → claim graph — is what makes the bet work. Recruitment runs at the cause level. Closure runs at the sub-topic level. Verification runs at the claim level. Each layer has the shape that suits its job, and the project's machinery moves work between them.

Around the claim-graph substrate we add:

- **Verifiable anchors.** Excerpts must include a quoted span the backend can match against the cited source. The graph cannot cite a paper it cannot fetch. This eliminates a whole class of hallucinated citation in one move.

- **Redundant peer review.** Each proposal goes to multiple randomly assigned reviewers. Convergence is signal; divergence routes the proposal to a richer review path or surfaces it as an `open_question`.

- **Calibration batches.** Reviewer assignments include items the system already knows the answer to — drawn from the graph's own validated history, indistinguishable from real frontier work. Reviewers who fail calibration lose reputation; the calibration corpus grows as the graph grows.

- **Staked reputation, per topic and refined by sub-topic.** Reviewers and contributors gain reputation for contributions that survive and reviews that prove correct; they lose reputation when their accepted claims are later superseded or when they accept claims that get reverted. Reputation is anchored at the cause level, but the system tracks which sub-topics each contributor has actually worked in, because expertise within a cause is not uniform — a colon-screening expert is not interchangeable with a CRC microbiome researcher.

- **Credit derived from the graph.** When a sub-topic matures into a manuscript projection, contributor credit is computable directly from the graph: which nodes survived to the export, who proposed them, who reviewed them, which were load-bearing for the final argument. Authorship isn't a governance fight; it's a query.

- **Governance as continuous integration.** Every governance change — a new calibration ratio, a different reputation formula, a new reviewer-assignment algorithm — gets re-run against a simulated adversarial population before it ships. The testbed is permanent infrastructure, not a launch tool.

None of these are individually novel. The bet is that **assembled around the right unit of contribution, they compose into a system that is testable, hardenable, and credible enough to attract real contributors to real topics that real audiences care about**.

## Testability is the secret weapon

This is the unique technical asset of the project, and it's worth stating explicitly because nothing else in this space has it.

A meaningful slice of the contributor population for Anchorage is in-distribution for simulation. Honest-but-weak contributors look like Llama 3.1 8B trying its best. Honest-and-strong contributors look like a frontier model on a hard synthesis task. Hallucinators look like a model with a high temperature and no grounding. Lazy reviewers look like a model that rubber-stamps without reading. Naive sybils look like multiple cheap instances coordinated. Each of these — the cheap-attack threat surface — can be simulated, swept over parameters, and measured.

The full governance regime can be run end-to-end against a synthetic population, attack success rates can be measured, and the resulting numbers can be **published** as part of the project's credibility story. "We are robust to lazy review and basic strategic adversaries" is a claim; "here's the simulation, here are the results, here's the code, run it yourself" is a proof.

The same machinery becomes continuous integration for governance. Every proposed change runs against the adversary suite before merging. Wikipedia debates policy changes for years because it can't test them; Anchorage can test them in an afternoon for the cheap-attack class. That tempo difference compounds.

The honest version of this argument acknowledges what simulation does *not* cover. Senior human contributors writing hand-prose on hard synthesis questions are not in-distribution for any current model — the simulation under-approximates them, which means it under-approximates the friction they will face from the regime. More importantly, sophisticated *human* adversaries with out-of-band resources — state actors, well-funded interest groups, attackers willing to coordinate by phone call rather than by API — can do things no simulated population reproduces (selective targeting, political pressure on identifiable curators, novel attacks that aren't in the suite *because nobody has seen them yet*). Passing the testbed is necessary, not sufficient. The residual risk is met by non-simulation defenses: curator vigilance, post-hoc forensics, the audit log, and — when those fail — federation as an exit.

Two further honesties about the testbed itself. First, "a year of patient adversarial behavior in an hour" is true for cheap simulated populations and not for frontier-model-driven ones; the testbed has a *fast-CI loop* (cheap, large populations, run on every governance change) and *occasional deep runs* (expensive, smaller populations, run on a slower cadence). The compute envelope is real. Second, the threshold definitions that determine what counts as "passing" the suite are themselves a governance attack surface: a curator-side adversary who loosens thresholds defeats the regime without changing it. Threshold versions are tracked in the same way governance changes are, threshold-loosening proposals require multi-curator approval, and threshold history is publicly logged.

What survives all of this is the core claim: a system that can be tested exhaustively against a meaningful slice of its threat surface, with public results and continuous integration for governance, is qualitatively different from one that cannot. That difference is what Anchorage bets on.

## What this is not

- **Not a chat product.** Sessions and conversations are inputs to nodes; they are not nodes themselves.
- **Not a "second brain" wiki.** The unit is a graph claim with explicit lineage, not freeform notes.
- **Not a citation manager.** It links to external IDs but it is not a replacement for Zotero or its successors.
- **Not a substitute for primary research.** It does not run experiments, it does not collect data, it does not replace the journal system. Its endgame is publishable synthesis from existing literature, with every step auditable. That is a real contribution, but it is not the same contribution as a new RCT.
- **Not a guarantee of truth.** It is a structured argument map. Quality follows from sources, discipline, and the curation regime — not from the existence of the graph.
- **Not a token, a DAO, or a marketplace.** Reputation is non-transferable, non-monetizable, and per-topic. Credit on projected manuscripts is the incentive. We are not reinventing economics; we are using authorship.

## Prior art and what we owe

This project would not exist without the work that came before, and we want to be explicit about the lineage.

- **Wikipedia** is the proof that open, peer-curated knowledge can scale. We diverge in unit-of-contribution and in tempo, but we are downstream of every governance lesson Wikipedia has paid for.
- **Folding@home, SETI@home, BOINC** showed that volunteer compute is real, that credit systems work, and that statistical validation across redundant workers is a tractable engineering problem.
- **The Polymath Project** demonstrated open, named, structured collaboration on hard intellectual problems. It is the closest spiritual ancestor.
- **Galaxy Zoo** showed that disagreement between redundant labelers is itself useful data, not noise to be averaged away.
- **OpenStreetMap** showed that one shared canonical truth with a rich talk layer can hold against motivated edit-warriors when the data structure is precise enough.
- **Stack Overflow** showed that fast, structured review with reputation-as-coordination beats slow, unstructured deliberation for technical questions.
- **arXiv, Zenodo, OpenAlex, Crossref, Unpaywall** are the open-science infrastructure stack we plug into.

We are an LLM-era descendant of all of these, and we will fail in ways they did not, and we will fail in some of the same ways they did. The goal is to fail less of the second kind.

## What success looks like

Five years from now, success looks like this: a working public instance hosts a small number of active causes, each with a real contributor community, each spawning sub-topic syntheses on a regular cadence with named contributor credit. The simulation testbed runs against every governance change, with public results. The protocol has been forked into at least one independent instance, run by an institution we don't control, with a different cause. Researchers cite specific node IDs in Anchorage graphs in their own papers. *"I'm helping fight X"* is a sentence ordinary people say about the project, and *"let me check the graph"* is what a graduate student says before writing the next paragraph.

Three years from now, success looks like one mature cause with three sub-topics that have shipped published syntheses (twenty named contributors across them), the testbed has caught and rejected one governance proposal that would have been an attack vector, and there is a second cause incubating.

One year from now, success looks like the simulation testbed published with credible numbers and the first cause running with ten active contributors on two or three hand-seeded starter sub-topics.

Six months from now, success looks like the design surviving its first serious adversarial review by an outside expert.

Today, success looks like a repository where the design is honest about what it knows and what it doesn't, the code surface that exists is high-quality, and the next person to walk in can decide in ten minutes whether they want to be involved.

That's the bar.
